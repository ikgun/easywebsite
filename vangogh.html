Van Gogh

Vincent van Gogh lived a life as turbulent as his skies — born in 1853 in the Netherlands, he wandered through dreams and despair in search of meaning. He worked as a preacher, a bookseller, a teacher, never quite fitting into the world’s frame. It wasn’t until his late twenties that he turned to painting, and in just a decade, he created over 2,000 works, each pulsing with raw emotion. Struggling with mental illness and loneliness, he found solace in nature and art — painting fields, flowers, and stars with a fevered, tender hand. Though he died by his own hand at 37, nearly unknown, his soul now lives eternal in every sunflower, every swirling sky.

His palette spoke when words failed — cobalt nights, golden wheat, and blazing blooms echoing the chaos within. In Arles, he dreamed of an artist’s haven, painting the Yellow House and his humble room with quiet hope. His friendship with Gauguin ended in madness and a severed ear, yet even in the asylum at Saint-Rémy, he created with unrelenting passion. Starry Night was born there — a cry into the cosmos, a glimpse of infinity through tortured eyes. Van Gogh gave the world not polished perfection, but aching truth. And though he walked alone in life, he now stands among the giants, his legacy a reminder that beauty often grows from broken places.
